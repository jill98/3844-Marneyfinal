History and Origins

The VT-Shaped Individual is a concept that has been in development at Virginia Tech in order to prepare their students to solve global problems through collaboration and contextual understanding.

The Components of the Model

The VT-Shaped Student is a Virginia Tech-originated student model that promotes a bountiful and diverse academic experience to ready students for global problem-solving. This is accomplished through deep understanding in chosen field of study (vertical line of T), diversifying areas of knowledge (horizontal line of T), and experiencing a wide variety of learning environments to enhance collaboration and intersection between talents (the V).

Thanassis Rikakis and VT's Plan

Thanassis Rikakis was Virginia Tech's first choice when looking for a Provost, believing that they needed the ex-Vice Provost for art and digital design at Carnegie Mellon to revitalize the Liberal Arts Program. Rikakis initially said no, believing that his artistic passions had no place at a school of engineering. But then he was inspired to create a student model at Virginia Tech to encourage intersection between subjects: the VT-Shaped Model. Rikakis wanted to bring a Renaissance way of thinking to Virginia Tech, with students jumping “from art to science as Galileo did, and from science to art as Leonardo da Vinci did.”

Liberal Arts and the Enhanced T

Initially, the T solely represented the combination of depth and width of knowledge. However, the Virginia Tech enhancement of the T was developed through cross-cutting a curriculum of technology with all available majors, particularily those with the Liberal Arts College. This ultimately allows the majority of Virginia Tech students to graduate with 21st century technology expertise, which they will then integrate their newfound knowledge of technology into their career of choice.

The Additional V

The V enriches the model through large-scale multi-function partnerships between the college community and respective potential industries. This component brings about both informal communal learning and guided experiential learning through opportunities like internships, research, philanthropies, and education abroad.

Enhanced T

Before taking the course Writing and Digital Media, I only taught myself the bare minimum of computer applications to skate by. Since the terms patience and attention to details didn't exist in my vocabulary, there didn't seem to be any point trying to learn the confusing art of coding, so I thought Why bother? What I had yet to realize, though, was how much I could thrive as a student with even just a basic knowledge of HTML and CSS. Coding is inherently intuitive and easy to commit to memory. To those who aren't initiually passionate about technological expertise, or to those who aren't detail-oriented, it will be a frustrating and draining process at the start. Here are some sources (with links!) that can help with the process:

  • Lynda.com: Tutorial videos for all website design aspect (HTML, CSS, Markdown, Java, etc.)
  • GitHub.com: Website platform for easy projects with HTML, CSS, and Markdown.
  • Commonmark.org: Concise and informative practice platform and tutorial for Markdown.
  • W3schools.com: Enormous compilation site that has tutorials, cheats, and practice problems.

    • The goal of this website will be to create a concise aid of all the information these sites share. I want to create a practice platform and a simple explanation of HTML, CSS, and Markdown for students who wish to start coding who aren't naturally drawn to the area of study. Ideally, this would be the opening page, and their would be a navigation bar that would link to practice platforms for different topics.
